How To Save Your
Childs Teeth?

Home Blog Article

Keeping your child's milk teeth healthy early in life is essential to their long-term well-being and future oral health. Milk teeth act as natural space maintainers for permanent teeth; any damage to them can directly impact permanent teeth development.

"Parents play the most critical role in building healthy dental habits for their children. Start early, make oral care fun, and visit the dentist regularly."

A) Brushing Techniques for Children

  • Parental Supervision: Parents should brush their child’s teeth twice daily until the child is old enough to hold and control a toothbrush properly.
  • Toothpaste Control: Pay close attention to the amount of toothpaste used (a smear for toddlers, pea-sized for older children). Ensure that the child does not swallow the toothpaste.
  • Spitting Habits: Teach your child to properly spit out the toothpaste after brushing and gargle thoroughly with water rather than swallowing it.
  • Gargling after Meals: Make it a habit for your child to gargle with plain water after every meal, especially after consuming sweet or sticky foods.

B) Developing a Child-Friendly Diet

  • Limit Sticky Foods: Avoid giving chocolates, candies, and sticky foods frequently. Replace snacks like chips and cookies with fresh fruits, green leafy vegetables, and raw vegetables (like carrots or cucumbers), which act as natural teeth-cleansing agents.
  • No Bedtime Sugars: Avoid giving sticky foods or sweetened milk in the night. Sugar residue left on the teeth overnight creates a breeding ground for bacteria, causing rapid tooth decay.
  • Night Bottle Care: If your child is bottle-fed at night, always wipe their teeth and gums with a clean, damp cloth after the feed.
  • Breastfeeding: Breastfeed the child up to two years to promote healthy jaw development and immune support.

C) Regular Dental Monitoring at Home

  • Check your child’s teeth regularly. Watch for any tiny black spots, signs of decay, or cavities.
  • As soon as you notice a suspect spot, consult a pediatric dentist immediately. Taking precautions early saves the child from pain and extensive procedures.
  • Schedule a routine dental checkup every 6 months. Remember, prevention is always better than cure.

D) Preparing Your Child for the Dentist

  • Avoid Fear Tactics: Never threaten your child with statements like "I will take you to the doctor and get an injection" if they misbehave. This builds fear and dental anxiety.
  • Filter Dental Stories: Never discuss any personal bad dental experiences or painful dental stories in front of your children. Doing so makes them apprehensive and uncooperative in the clinic.

By following these basic guidelines, you can ensure your child grows up with a cavity-free, healthy, and confident smile.
